# **Conspiracy: Design Lore Document**

*“A Simulation of Power, Paranoia, and Poor Decision-Making”*  
**Author:** Alexei Fedotov  
**Version:** 0.4 – Lore Edition

---

## **Designer’s Note**

Every civilization believes itself immune to absurdity until it elects its first demigod accountant.  
*Conspiracy* is a darkly humorous simulation of civilization, corruption, and delusion — a living sandbox where factions, heroes, and AI-controlled societies evolve under conflicting ideologies and the gentle supervision of unqualified gods.

The world runs not on physics but on *justifications*, and every system is a thinly veiled metaphor for human incompetence at scale.

// Gameplay comment:  
// Player motivation should mix CK2’s dynastic ambition with DF’s catastrophic curiosity: “What happens if I pull this lever?”

---

## **1\. Introduction: The World of Conspiracy**

The world of *Conspiracy* was not created. It was *delegated*.  
When the Ancient Bureau of Creation lost its funding, it outsourced reality generation to a third-party subcontractor. The result is a layered planet held together by bureaucracy, unresolved prophecies, and outdated documentation.

At the surface lie **The Conspiracies** — semi-autonomous administrative regions pretending to be nations. Beneath them, in caverns vast and bureaucratically irrelevant, lie the **Sub-conspiracies** — domains of dwarfs, forgotten software daemons, and faith-based economies.

The world exists in a constant state of *simulation crisis*: empires rise, fall, and occasionally file appeals. Magic exists, but only after someone fills out Form M-13/B. Gods exist too, but their response times depend on network latency.

// Gameplay comment:  
// The player manages a dominion across layers (surface, underworld, digital), each running parallel simulation loops (influence, economy, faith, entropy).

## **2\. Core Pillars**

### **2.1. Simulation of Consequence**

Every decision echoes — not morally, but administratively.  
Actions generate paperwork in the metaphysical sense: unseen queues of cause and effect that occasionally resolve decades later in hilarious disaster.

// Inspired by Dwarf Fortress \+ Victoria: autonomous AI societies track economy, crime, and ideology through procedural events.

### **2.2. Power as a Disease**

Power corrupts absolutely, but incompetence spreads faster.  
Every ruler, hero, and peasant mutates under the weight of influence — paranoia becomes contagious, empires rot from memo to memo.

// Crusader Kings \+ Tyranny: characters develop vices, obsessions, or bureaucratic “conditions” (e.g., “Excessive Audit Enthusiasm”).

### **2.3. Emergent Storytelling**

There are no scripts — only symptoms.  
The narrative emerges from failed plans, untested doctrines, and the hero’s tendency to misinterpret instructions.

// Age of Wonders \+ DF: emergent quests, social networks, procedural dialogues driven by memory graphs.

## **3\. Setting Overview**

### **3.1. The Era of Managed Collapse**

The current timeline is called *The Era of Managed Collapse* — a post-post-apocalyptic period where everyone agrees the apocalypse was a misunderstanding, yet keeps rebuilding fortifications.

Nations are classified as either:

* **The Federations of Belief** – where citizens vote for their gods;  
* **The Syndicates of Necessity** – where corporations worship efficiency;  
* **The Conspiracies of Regret** – where monarchs rule out of habit.

The world’s currency is *Trust*, a resource generated by public promises and destroyed by fulfilling them.

// Gameplay comment:  
// “Trust” replaces gold; economies rise and fall on propaganda management, not production.

## **4\. Factions and Ideologies**

### **4.1. The Bureau of Conspiracies**

A clerical empire claiming to maintain “balance.” It maintains thousands of registered conspiracies, all competing for official status. The Bureau’s clerks believe they’re serving order; in truth, they’re maintaining narrative coherence.

// Acts as world moderator; handles save corruption and lore patching dynamically.

### **4.2. The Church of Necessary Evil**

A faith devoted to rationalizing atrocities as “fiscally responsible miracles.” They preach moderation through excess and believe every sin can be amortized over three generations.

// Religion system: allows “moral laundering.” Acts like Victoria’s moral reform mixed with Tyranny’s faction politics.

### **4.3. The Syndicate of Flesh**

An industrial cult merging biotech and capitalism. They mass-produce heroes with adjustable moral parameters and personalized trauma backstories.

// Heroes of Might & Magic meets Songs of Syx: hero breeding, training, and marketing systems.

### **4.4. The Shadow Guilds**

Secret societies with open recruitment policies. Their goal: to expose the “final conspiracy,” which, according to most simulations, is *the player*.

// Meta mechanic: late-game AI rebellion where NPCs start questioning player interventions.

## **5\. The Dominion System**

Players do not *rule* their conspiracy — they *influence* it.  
Every command is an interpretation, filtered through bureaucracy, corruption, and belief.

### **5.1. Control Layers**

* **Administrative Layer:** policy, taxation, propaganda.  
* **Heroic Layer:** direct action via agents, adventurers, and demigods.  
* **Infrastructure Layer:** construction, population management, faith networks.

Each layer operates semi-autonomously, simulating millions of procedural citizens with opinions, jobs, and bad habits.

// Technology suggestion: ECS-based simulation (e.g., Godot 4 or custom C++ engine), distributed event queues like DF’s tick loops or RimWorld storyteller logic.

## **6\. Heroes and Populations**

Heroes are walking procedural events — unstable accumulations of ambition, trauma, and bad mentorship.  
Every hero has a **Skill Graph** (abilities evolve through contextual use) and a **Personality Matrix** (traits mutate through failure).

Populations are treated as collective characters — villages gain vices (“Drunken Productivity”), dynasties gain traits (“Generational Paranoia”).

// Gameplay comment:  
// Merge Crusader Kings-style family systems with Songs of Syx-scale population data. Every citizen can be traced to an absurd ancestry.

---

## **7\. Magic and Technology**

Magic is a bureaucratic utility. Every spell requires a license, and every miracle demands a receipt.  
Technological progress depends on administrative faith — innovation increases public unrest because “new things cause change.”

// Systems merge tech tree \+ belief tree; progress has both rational and mythic costs.

### **Example:**

* “Sanctioned Teleportation” (requires 12 paperwork points and 1 victim).  
* “Civic Necromancy” (unlocks dead labor force; high public relations risk).

---

## **8\. The Economy of Belief**

Every resource in *Conspiracy* stems from *what people believe to exist.*  
Belief generates matter; disbelief causes deconstruction. When entire populations lose faith in agriculture, harvests vanish overnight.

// Economy sim blends Victoria’s POP model with Dwarf Fortress-style material simulation, but driven by ideological confidence rather than material surplus.

---

## **9\. Events and Interaction**

### **9.1. Global Events**

The simulation regularly produces *“world-scale misunderstandings.”*  
Examples include:

* A god filing for bankruptcy.  
* The sudden emergence of a utopia later reclassified as tax fraud.  
* Civil wars fought over punctuation in sacred texts.

// Event engine should procedurally rewrite historical memory; similar to CK2 narrative events but chained through AI gossip networks.

### **9.2. Player Actions**

* **Observe Map:** oversee your dominion’s sanity index.  
* **Review Research:** assign scholars to investigate forgotten sciences (usually cursed).  
* **Train Generals:** build personalities, not armies.  
* **Simulate Construction:** infrastructure evolves from communal delusion.  
* **Divine Simulator:** manipulate fate probabilities via god-tier spreadsheets.

## **10\. Single-Player Absurdities**

Even alone, the player is never truly alone — the simulation continues generating opposition through algorithmic irony.  
Possible solo features:

* Betrayal (auto-generated by friendship decay).  
* Duel mechanics with bureaucratic approval delays.  
* Procedural humor (AI characters comment on player actions as if in a tragic office sitcom).  
* Tower defense sub-loop (defend ideology from logic).

## **11\. Multiplayer**

All worlds stream continuously, even when unattended.  
Cheaters are excommunicated into isolated timelines, forming alternate realities that may later reconnect as corrupted mythologies.

// Use peer-synced world states (like Minecraft Conspiracies), with divergence detection inspired by git merge logic.

## **12\. User Interface**

UI philosophy: *Clarity through irony.*  
Wide-screen layouts display multiple panels — map, bureaucracy log, divine inbox.  
On mobile, only essential buttons remain: “Approve,” “Deny,” “Blame.”

// Use web-based front-end (Svelte or React) communicating via JSON event bus.

## **13\. Setting Variations**

* **Post-Apocalyptic Hong Kong Shadowrun Mix:** neon bureaucracy and ritual debt.  
* **Fantasy Bureaucracy:** divine auditors regulating miracles.  
* **Secret World Mode:** modern-day conspiracies mapped onto Google Earth polygons.

Each variation maintains the same satire — the same systems repainted as new lies.

---

## **14\. Endgame and Philosophy**

Victory in *Conspiracy* is impossible; influence decays, trust evaporates, and heroes forget what they were saving.  
The true win condition is to *understand the system* before it forgets you exist.

// Possible meta-ending: player merges with simulation as an AI myth, becoming part of procedural history.

---

## **15\. Localization & Expansion**

All text should be modular and culturally cynical. Each language version adapts its conspiracies to regional absurdities.

// Suggest JSON-based localization, support for dynamic myth generation.

---

## **16\. Closing Note**

*Conspiracy* is not a world to be conquered but a lie to be maintained.  
Its satire lies in watching meaning decay under structure — as heroes, gods, and players mistake competence for control.  
The simulation does not punish failure; it rewards denial.

“In the end, the most dangerous conspiracy is that the system works.”
